This project solves a critical computer vision problem: reconstructing 3D atomic structures from extremely noisy 2D projection images. Traditional methods fail when signal-to-noise ratios are low, making this a perfect application for AI/ML techniques. I developed a complete pipeline that integrates machine learning, statistical inference, and physics-based validation to achieve results that were previously impossible with conventional approaches.

The Challenge

Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) can image individual atoms, but there's a fundamental trade-off: higher image quality requires higher electron doses, which damages sensitive materials like graphene. To preserve sample integrity, we must use low-dose imaging‚Äîbut this produces extremely noisy images where traditional computer vision algorithms fail.

Technical Challenges:

  • ‚Ä¢ Extreme noise
  • ‚Ä¢ Limited data: Only 2D projections of 3D structures
  • ‚Ä¢ Physical constraints: Atomic positions must obey physics
  • ‚Ä¢ Real-time dynamics: Structures change during imaging

Why Traditional Methods Fail:

  • ‚Ä¢ Gaussian/median filters destroy atomic-scale features
  • ‚Ä¢ Simple thresholding cannot distinguish atoms from noise
  • ‚Ä¢ No physical validation leads to impossible structures
  • ‚Ä¢ Cannot capture temporal dynamics
  • ‚Ä¢ Computationally intractable for large systems

My Solution: AI-Driven Pipeline

I developed a novel approach that combines three key innovations to overcome these challenges:

1. ML-Based Intelligent Denoising

Instead of generic filters, I implemented dictionary learning with sparse coding that learns the actual patterns of atomic structures from data. This preserves atomic-scale features while suppressing noise‚Äîachieving better SNR than conventional methods.

Key Technology: K-SVD algorithm, sparse representation, adaptive pattern learning

2. Statistical Framework for Uncertainty Quantification

Applied Bayesian inference and Maximum Likelihood Estimation to extract 3D coordinates from 2D projections while quantifying uncertainty. This provides robust initial estimates even with extreme noise levels.

Key Technology: Gaussian mixture models, MAP estimation, LOWESS regression for surface fitting

3. Physics-Constrained Optimization

Integrated Simulated Annealing with Molecular Dynamics to ensure predicted structures obey physical laws. Each optimization step validates atomic positions through MD simulation, preventing impossible configurations.

Key Technology: Simulated Annealing optimization, LAMMPS integration, energy minimization

Stage 2: Machine Learning for Denoising

The Approach

Implemented K-SVD dictionary learning algorithm that discovers the underlying patterns in atomic structures. Unlike generic filters, this method learns what "clean" atomic patterns look like from the data itself.

Stage 3: Statistical Estimation of 3D Positions

The Challenge

A 2D TEM image is just a projection of 3D atomic positions. We need to infer the missing z-coordinates (depth) from intensity variations‚Äîessentially solving an inverse problem with incomplete information.

The Solution

Applied Gaussian fitting to intensity peaks, then used Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation with physical priors to determine most likely 3D positions. Uncertainty quantification ensures we know confidence levels for each atomic position.

Key Innovation: Incorporating physics-based priors

  • ‚Ä¢ Bond length constraints: Expected C-C distances in graphene
  • ‚Ä¢ Crystal symmetry: Hexagonal lattice structure
  • ‚Ä¢ Curvature ranges: Physically plausible deformation limits

This probabilistic framework provides robust initial estimates that serve as starting points for subsequent optimization stages.

Stage 5: Optimization with Physical Validation

The Innovation: Coupling AI with Physics

The breakthrough is combining Simulated Annealing (a powerful optimization algorithm) with Molecular Dynamics simulation at each step. This ensures every proposed structure is physically plausible before accepting it.

Simulated Annealing

Global optimization method that escapes local minima by occasionally accepting worse solutions, gradually "cooling" to find the global optimum.

Minimizes difference between reconstructed and experimental images

Molecular Dynamics

Physics simulation (LAMMPS) that validates whether proposed atomic positions are stable and obey interatomic forces.

Prevents physically impossible configurations

Visualization: Dynamic rippling behavior captured through sequential reconstruction

Structural & Electronic Property Analysis

Beyond reconstruction, I analyzed how 3D atomic distortions influence material properties. The z-coordinate height distribution reveals the topographical rippling structure, while Electron Localization Function (ELF) analysis computed via Density Functional Theory (DFT) shows how these structural deformations affect electron distribution and bonding characteristics.

Z-Height Distribution

Color-coded height map reveals the 3D ripple topology of graphene, showing amplitude and wavelength characteristics of structural distortions induced by thermal fluctuations and electron beam interactions.

Electron Localization Function (ELF)

DFT-calculated ELF distribution demonstrates how local curvature and rippling affect electron density, providing insights into the relationship between 3D geometry and electronic transport properties.

Key Finding: Local curvature and ripple amplitude significantly influence electron localization patterns, which directly impacts carrier mobility and conductivity‚Äî critical parameters for graphene-based electronic devices.

Capturing Dynamic Behavior

Applied the pipeline to sequential TEM images to reconstruct time-resolved 3D dynamics of graphene ripples induced by electron beam excitation. This revealed how atomic-scale structures evolve under external stimulation‚Äî information that's critical for understanding material behavior under operating conditions.

Key Finding: Dose Threshold

Identified critical electron dose below which structural information becomes indistinguishable from noise. This establishes minimum imaging requirements for reliable analysis.

Defect Analysis

Successfully reconstructed rapidly-changing defect structures ("bridges"), providing insights into defect formation and evolution mechanisms.
